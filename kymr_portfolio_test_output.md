## Strategic Emphasis

Kymera's partnership structure reveals conviction more clearly than its pipeline slide. The company kept KT-621 (STAT6, $20B+ TAM) and KT-579 (IRF5, $45B+ TAM) wholly-owned while handing IRAK4 to Sanofi for $150M upfront and CDK2 to Gilead for $85M. That's $235M in non-dilutive cash funding the two programs management believes will define the company — enough to extend runway into 2029 without touching the equity market. This is a deliberate bet structure: Sanofi and Gilead are paying for the right to develop the programs Kymera considers lower-priority, while Kymera retains full economics on its two highest-conviction assets.

KT-621 is the proof-of-concept vehicle, not the endgame. The Phase 1b open-label data showing >90% STAT6 degradation and Dupixent-matching biomarker responses exists to validate the platform thesis: that oral degraders can achieve biologic-like target elimination. BROADEN2 (Phase 2b, placebo-controlled, EASI-75 at Week 16) is the real gate. Everything downstream — KT-579 in lupus, the asthma expansion, COPD, EoE — is priced contingent on BROADEN2 confirming what the open-label suggested.

## Key Tension Points

**The BROADEN2 binary.** If BROADEN2 misses EASI-75 at Week 16 → the "oral degrader = injectable biologic" thesis collapses → KT-579 and KT-485 lose their platform halo (why would IRF5 degradation translate if STAT6 didn't?) → market reprices KYMR from platform premium to single-asset salvage. The bear case here has teeth: AD carries a 25-35% placebo response rate, the Phase 1b was open-label with only 29 days of treatment, and Dupilumab trials ran 16 weeks. Kymera has never shown durability data. **Probability: moderate.** The >90% target degradation is mechanistically compelling — you're eliminating the protein, not partially inhibiting it — but open-label dermatology data has a history of disappointing under blinded conditions.

**KT-579 in SLE — genetically right, clinically brutal.** IRF5 gain-of-function variants are among the strongest GWAS signals in lupus, and head-to-head preclinical data against Saphnelo (anifrolumab) and Benlysta (belimumab) showed superior survival and kidney protection. The scientific case is strong. But SLE has a ~70% clinical failure rate historically — heterogeneous disease, unreliable endpoints (SRI-4), and patients on complex background therapy that masks treatment effects. KT-579 has zero human data. Phase 1 healthy volunteer data is expected 2H 2026, which will answer the first question (is target degradation achievable and safe?) but not the second (does it translate in lupus patients?). If Phase 1 is clean but the eventual SLE study fails → $45B TAM narrative evaporates → Kymera is a single-franchise company with one wholly-owned clinical asset.

**Competitive timing on STAT6.** Kymera's BROADEN2 readout is mid-2027 — that's 15+ months away. Dupixent ($13B franchise) isn't standing still: Sanofi is expanding into COPD, EoE, CRSwNP, BPAI. More critically, JAK inhibitors (Rinvoq, Cibinqo) are approved and gaining share despite boxed warnings. If JAK safety concerns dissipate over time with more real-world data, the convenience argument for an oral degrader weakens. Kymera's edge is targeting STAT6 specifically (vs broad JAK pathway inhibition), which should mean a cleaner safety profile — but that's unproven in controlled trials.

## Portfolio Gaps

**Duration gap.** The most glaring absence in Kymera's data package is durability. KT-621 Phase 1b was 29 days. Dupixent's registration trials were 16 weeks with long-term extension data. Before BROADEN2, there is no evidence that STAT6 degradation maintains efficacy beyond one month. For a drug competing against a biologic with 8+ years of real-world evidence, this matters.

**Geographic white space.** No disclosed regulatory strategy outside the US for any program. Atopic dermatitis is a $5B+ market in Japan alone, and Dupixent faces biosimilar exposure by ~2030. An early partnership or filing strategy in Asia could secure positioning ahead of the BROADEN2 readout.

**Combination strategy.** No disclosed plans to combine KT-621 with JAK inhibitors, anti-IL-13 antibodies, or topical agents. In moderate-to-severe AD, combination therapy is increasingly standard. A combination trial would be low-cost and high-signal for differentiation.
